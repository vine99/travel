Staying near the Vatican

by Daniel Vineyard
MARCH 27, 2024 | Rome, Italy

In the heart of Rome, just a stone's throw from the imposing walls of the Vatican, there existed a quaint, sun-dappled apartment on the top floor of an ancient building, whose history whispered through its ivy-clad facade.

In the early Roman light, near the Vatican, I started the day. Rome was waking. The streets, narrow and cobbled, had the colors of the earth and history.

I walked early, to see the city before it filled. My steps on the cobblestones took me to a small café in the shadow of St. Peter's. The smell of coffee and the hiss of milk being steamed filled the air. I sat outside, where the morning was still cool and the city's sounds mixed with bits of conversations.

With a cappuccino, thick and smooth, in front of me, I watched. A scooter passed, quick and close, a dance with the ancient streets that spoke of a modern Rome still entwined.

The sun moved higher, shadows playing across the stones. I joined the line at the Vatican, a mix of people from everywhere, drawn here to this place of faith and art. Inside, it was cooler, and voices dropped to whispers. Looking up at the Sistine Chapel, the figures painted by Michelangelo seemed to move in the half-light, alive with stories of the divine and human struggle.

As evening came, the sky turned to pink and gold, and I sat by the Tiber. The city shifted into night, lights reflecting on the water. The dome of St. Peter's stood out.

Rome, with its layers of beauty and life, had shown itself in a single day. But it was in the small things—the coffee, the craft, the quiet by the river—that I found the heart of the city. Rome was a place of moments, each one a part of the whole, alive and timeless and it felt so alive and amazing.

In Rome's early light, close to the Vatican, I walked alone. The city was quiet, awakening. Streets, narrow and shadowed by buildings colored like the earth, were mine to explore.

I found a café near St. Peter's, its scent of coffee strong and inviting. Sitting outside, the morning air carried bits of conversations, a lifeblood of the city. I drank a cappuccino, I had never had such a taste as this before.

Scooters passed, their riders weaving through streets with an ease born of necessity. Rome lived in these moments, ancient yet ever moving.

By midmorning, I joined the line at the Vatican. It was long, filled with people from everywhere, all here to see what has been preserved. Inside, the cool air and the scale.

An Evening in Rome
As evening came, with the sky turning pink and gold, I sat by the Tiber. The city shifted into night, lights reflecting on the water. The dome of St. Peter's was a distant, glowing presence.

Rome revealed itself not in the grandeur of its monuments, but in these small moments. The morning coffee, the touch of leather, the quiet by the river. It was a city of contrasts, of the eternal and the everyday, each piece a part of the whole.

The evening deepened, drawing a curtain over the city's day. The streets, once awash with the footsteps of the day's beginning, now echoed with the serene quiet of night. Rome, timeless and ever-changing, lay before me, a narrative of history and the present intertwined.

Dinner was at a place I stumbled upon, hidden in an alleyway where the walls leaned in close, as if to share the secret of this spot. The menu was simple, handwritten, promising dishes made with ingredients whose origins were as local as the soil and sea could provide. I chose pasta, its sauce rich with tomatoes that tasted of the sun, and wine, red and robust, speaking of the earth it came from.

In my room, the sounds of the city were a distant murmur, a lullaby of sorts. Rome, with its endless layers, had offered me a glimpse into its heart. It was a city where the past and present lived side by side, where every stone and corner had a story, and where the simplest pleasures—a cup of coffee, a plate of pasta.

As I drifted to sleep, I thought of what tomorrow might bring, another day in this eternal city, another page in its endless story. Rome was a teacher of sorts, showing that life, in all its complexity, was to be lived fully, tasted deeply, and remembered always.

The next morning, I rose with the sun. Rome at dawn holds a clarity, a pause before the day unfolds. I walked towards the Campo de' Fiori. The market there was setting up, stalls bursting with colors and smells. Tomatoes, bright and ripe, olives glistening like polished stones, and the sharp tang of fresh cheese filled the air. The vendors, with their easy banter, reminded me of fishermen preparing for a day.

In the afternoon
In the afternoon, I found myself at the Pantheon. Its dome, a marvel of ancient engineering, captured the sky in a perfect circle of light. Inside, the coolness and the sense of something greater than ourselves enveloped me. People moved in and out of beams of light, like actors.

The day ended on the Spanish Steps. As the sun dipped below the horizon, the steps became a canvas of shadows and light, the chatter of tourists a backdrop to my thoughts. Rome, a city of layers, each day peeling back to reveal more. It was in these moments, between the grandiosity of its past and the intimacy of its present, that I found a story unfolding, one that was mine and yet belonged to the city itself.

Rome wasn't just a place of historical marvels; it was a living, breathing entity, where every street corner, every whispered conversation, every burst of laughter in a crowded market, wove into the fabric of now. As I sat there, the city's heartbeat became my own, a rhythm dictated not by the ticking of a clock but by the unfolding of life itself.

Lunch was in a small piazza, at a table under the shade of an orange tree. The food was simple, pasta with a sauce that spoke of the sun and the earth, accompanied by bread and wine. Around me, life unfolded in a series of vignettes: a child chasing a ball, a couple leaning close, their conversation a private murmur.

After the market, I ventured towards the Trastevere, crossing the river on a bridge where artists displayed their work. The Tiber was calm, its surface a mirror to the sky. Trastevere greeted me with its labyrinth of streets, each turn revealing a café, a hidden garden, or a wall thick with ivy. The sound of a guitar floated from an open window, a serenade to the morning.

Lunch was in a small piazza, at a table under the shade of an orange tree. The food was simple, pasta with a sauce that spoke of the sun and the earth, accompanied by bread and wine. Around me, life unfolded in a series of vignettes: a child chasing a ball, a couple leaning close, their conversation a private murmur, an old man reading a newspaper.

Until tomorrow, Rome...
